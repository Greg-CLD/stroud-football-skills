# Homepage Design Intent (Jules Football Skills)

## Quick summary
This homepage is intentionally designed **for young children first, and parents second**. It should feel like a **football-themed game board**: colourful, playful, bold, fun, energetic, friendly, and tactile. Future edits should **extend this style**, not replace it with a generic adult website look.

## Audience and experience intent

### Primary audience: young children
- The page should immediately feel exciting and easy for children to understand.
- Visuals should be big, obvious, and rewarding (clear sections, bold headings, strong buttons, emoji-supported cues).
- Interaction should feel playful and game-like, not technical.

### Secondary audience: parents
- Parents should still be able to quickly find practical items (for example, the Parent Zone link).
- Parent-focused content should remain clear and trustworthy, while still fitting the child-first visual system.

## Visual style principles to preserve

### 1) Colour system: bright and saturated
- Keep strong football-friendly colours (sky blue, grass green, sunny yellow, orange/red, deep blue).
- Prefer high-contrast section blocks over neutral/muted surfaces.
- Use colour to separate sections and signal actions.

### 2) Shape language: chunky, rounded, tactile
- Use rounded corners heavily (cards, badges, buttons, panels).
- Keep thick borders and visible depth.
- Preserve a sticker/trading-card/arcade-button feel.

### 3) Typography: bold and playful
- Preserve playful family choices and bold weights.
- Headings should feel loud and celebratory (uppercase and strong hierarchy where appropriate).
- Body copy should stay simple and readable, not dense.

### 4) Depth and emphasis
- Keep pronounced shadows and layered surfaces.
- Maintain clear separation between zones (hero, skills, media, reflections, parent zone).
- Prefer visually obvious hierarchy over subtle minimalism.

### 5) Imagery and icon language
- Keep football imagery active and colourful.
- Continue using emoji as part of labels, buttons, section titles, and card icons where helpful.
- Ensure imagery supports motion/energy (playing, action, practice moments).

## Interaction style
- Interactions should be simple, obvious, and immediate.
- Buttons should look pressable and rewarding (active/push feedback).
- Tabs/cards should feel lively, not flat.
- Motion can be playful (bounce/hover), but should remain clear and purposeful.
- Avoid abstract patterns that hide what to click.

## Content and copy tone
- Use short, upbeat, child-friendly sentences.
- Keep copy easy to scan.
- Maintain a positive, encouraging voice.
- Prefer football words children understand (practice, skills, goal, team, try again).
- Avoid formal, corporate, or overly serious wording.

## Must-preserve features
Unless there is a strong reason, keep these characteristics intact:
- Bright football colour palette and high contrast.
- Playful, bold heading treatment and clear section labels.
- Chunky rounded buttons/cards with strong shadows/borders.
- Strong visual hierarchy and section separation.
- Game-like, fun energy in interactions and layout.
- Child-first tone, with parent utility presented second.

## What to avoid
Future edits should avoid introducing:
- Muted or desaturated palettes.
- Corporate UI patterns.
- Minimalist adult SaaS styling.
- Flat grey cards and low-contrast surfaces.
- Generic modern web styling that removes personality.
- Overly serious/formal copy.
- Replacing playful imagery with bland placeholders.
- Reducing the tactile “pressable” feel of key actions.

## Practical implementation guidance for future Codex edits
- When adding a new section, match existing colour strength, rounded shapes, border thickness, and playful tone.
- When changing buttons or cards, keep them bold, high-contrast, and visibly pressable.
- When adding images, prefer lively, colourful football activity over static or sterile visuals.
- When adding new UI patterns, extend current conventions (badges, chunky buttons, card blocks, emoji cues) instead of redesigning from scratch.
- If a change improves accessibility, apply it in a way that preserves the same playful visual identity.

## Working rule (for future homepage edits)
**Before finalizing any homepage change, check: does it still look and feel like a fun, colourful football playground for young children? If not, revise until it does.**
